The Hall Effect Thruster

What would you think if you were told that today’s space travel relies on non-combustible fuel? You may be tempted to think this is science fiction, however this is exactly how the Hall Effect Thruster functions. Once a spacecraft is in space this invention applies about the same amount of force that you would feel holding a stack of 10 quarters, or a single tennis ball to the object. Over time in the frictionless void of space the spacecraft is slowly and continuously accelerated to extreme velocities; up to 200,000 mph! A single noble gas, typically Xenon which is particularly ionizable, is used as fuel in a process that is over twice as efficient as traditional chemical thrusters. Welcome to the world of Ion Propulsion.

What is a Hall Effect Thruster?

The Hall Effect can trace its name back to Mr. Edwin Herbert Hall, an American scientist who discovered the Hall Effect in 1879. This is a property that can be applied to electrically conductive material by a magnetic field. When there is current across a conductor in the presence of a magnetic field the moving charges migrate towards the surface of the material. Molecules with the opposite charge, positive or negative, outside the conductor then migrate to the conductor’s surface as a result. The Hall effect thrusters use this Hall Effect in order to ionize the Xenon Propellant which then due to the electric field is expelled from the thruster at high speeds.

The Hall Effect Thruster is an ion propulsion system. Xenon propellant is essentially fuel composed of Xenon gas. Although the Hall Effect Thrusters primarily use Xenon, other noble gasses can be used. This choice to use noble gasses as propellant is two-fold; First, a gaseous propellant weighs substantially less than either liquid or solid propellants. Second, noble gasses are inert meaning that if there is a fuel leak or malfunction within the propulsion system there is no chance of a fire or explosion occurring.

    Benefits of Electric Propulsion

  • Improved efficiency: Unique designs minimize energy loss to the discharge chamber, which improves discharge efficiency
  • Increased lifetime: HET lifetime is extended five times, from approximately 10,000 hours to more than 50,000 hours
  • Improved performance: Glenn's innovations result in a greater fraction of the electrical energy from the solar array being used to produce thrust, and a decrease in electrical energy being lost as waste heat radiated to space

Electric VS Chemical Propulsion

The debate of electric vs chemical propulsion is a space-based debate. This is because an electric thruster is not capable of delivering enough acceleration to launch a vehicle into space at this point in time. The amount of force they can apply is roughly equal to what is felt holding a stack of 10 quarters or 1 single tennis-ball. As a result all electric propulsion systems are dependent upon chemical propulsion to get them into space. Once we are in space however there are a number of advantages to electric propulsion over chemical propulsion.

Before the comparison begins it is necessary to point out that all forms of propulsion in space are only able to accelerate the vehicle in one single direction. This comes down to the laws of physics and the fact that force can only be applied to a single point at a time. Cars are able to change direction by changing the direction the front wheels are facing. During this change the engine is still causing the wheels to rotate applying force in all directions between the initial and final trajectories that the wheels and therefore the car is facing. In space the rocket engine just like a car’s engine applies its force in the direction that the craft is facing. Because there is no road to push against to steer a vehicle in space other engines must be present at different angles on the craft to align it with the desired direction of travel.

Now, on the topic of comparison of electric vs chemical propulsion first let us consider the solid-propellant based chemical thruster. Due to the fact that the chemical thruster will continuously burn using all its fuel raising a space vehicle’s velocity to a precise point is difficult to achieve. A very precise size of propellant would have to be fitted onto the craft and even then a number of unknown variables relating to its uniform composition and heat generation in combustion would affect the burn of the propellant and resultant acceleration of the craft. Ion thrusters on the other hand are able to regulate the amount of propellant released and therefore the amount of time they are active. Because of this they are able to achieve specific final velocities which is beneficial for the undertaking of more complex missions where specific velocities are required in order to achieve particular orbits of celestial bodies. Additionally, this allows for the craft to avoid unnecessary collisions with greater accuracy without having to deal with the variable of time in a continuous path of acceleration. Next, due to the heat generated in combustion sensitive scientific instruments may be affected, and once they are out in space their repair may be impossible. Electric thrusters to not generate a tremendous amount of heat, this coupled with the relatively low amount of force on the craft itself allows for sensitive scientific equipment to remain in a comparatively safe environment.

Comparing electric and liquid-based chemical thrusters is a different story. Both of these forms of propulsion are able to regulate their propellant enabling a finer degree of accuracy in their final velocities. Liquid chemical thrusters however suffer from having two different types of propellant onboard which greatly increases the overall mass of the craft. Additionally because both propellants are draining at specific rates the calculation of the force being applied to the craft at a specific time becomes complex. Electric thrusters use a single gaseous propellant which has considerably less weight than two liquid propellants. The force calculation of the craft is made much simpler through its use. Once again, the chemical propulsion imparts a large amount of heat in proximity to equipment which may have detrimental effects where the electric thruster does not.
